Spurred to Action

What catalyzed the Nonprofit Roundtable Series?

Beginning in 2020, a small group of funders in Atlanta began collaborating more deliberately to respond to the financial pressures nonprofits face. During the COVID-19 crisis, several of the foundations within the group coordinated funding and investment strategies, including seeding the Community Foundation for Greater Atlanta's Community Guarantee Pool, which helped unlock CDFI bridge lending to cover delayed government funding.

Subsequently, the group has closely monitored the tapering of pandemic-era funding streams, which have left many nonprofits facing increased demand with fewer public resources. In this vein, several foundations co-funded a supplemental state-level analysis as part of the 2025 Nonprofit Finance Fund National Survey.

Having years of "collective muscle building," this small group of funders sprang into action in early 2025, as the flurry of executive orders and related funding freezes created immediate threats to nonprofits and the communities they serve. After some discussion, the funders agreed to host a small roundtable series that brings together philanthropic leaders, nonprofit executives, CDFIs, and commercial lenders.

Roundtable Learnings

What did we learn during the roundtable sessions?

Roundtable Session 1 — Field Context

Atlanta's nonprofit sector finds itself in the eye of a perfect storm, as delayed reimbursements, uncertainty around key federal funding programs, growing expense levels, reserve draws, and escalating service demand converge to strain operations.

It's important to acknowledge that structural challenges with government reimbursements and funding are not new. Nor are they limited to federal dollars. Challenges around cash-flow timing have long been built into the reimbursable model of government funding, which nonprofits often refer to as the "float" between service delivery and payment receipt.

Even before recent federal budget uncertainties amplified delays, organizations routinely advanced payroll, rent, and program expenses while awaiting contract draws or grant reimbursements. Smaller agencies—without lines of credit or unrestricted reserves—sometimes waited 30 to 60 days for state or local payments, and reporting requirements could stretch that gap further.

Per the Nonprofit Finance Fund's 2022 State of the Nonprofit Sector Survey: About 45% of U.S. nonprofits report having less than three months (~90 days) of cash on hand for operating expenses.

Common Cash Flow Challenges — Use Cases

Use Case 1 Advancing or Bridging Reimbursable Funding

What we heard from nonprofits

A twenty-year-old homelessness-focused nonprofit has been routinely awarded federal grants through both the VA and HUD. Federal reimbursements routinely arrive 60–90 days after invoicing. Over the past year, the nonprofit has tapped its reserves and incurred expensive short-term credit to cover payroll and fixed costs. The ideal solution would be a multi-year, simple, renewable bridge loan or working LOC.

What the data says

A GrantStation survey highlights that organizations routinely tap lines of credit to cover payroll and core expenses "when cash flow is low" due to delayed reimbursements. NFF's 2022 Survey found that 55% of nonprofits reported needing external liquidity to manage timing gaps between service delivery and funding disbursements.

Use Case 2 Refinancing High-Cost Debt

What we heard from nonprofits

A mental-health nonprofit holds a legacy line of credit at double-digit interest rates, originally secured to tide over pandemic-era revenue dips. With upcoming reductions in government funding and flat donor support, the organization seeks to refinance into a lower-cost, longer-term facility. The ideal solution blends CDFI-subsidized pricing with a streamlined approval process that doesn't require additional collateral.

What the data says

Refinancing high-cost legacy debt is significant. In the wake of pandemic emergency borrowing, roughly 1 in 4 nonprofits carried lines of credit with double-digit interest rates well into 2023, according to Abrigo. These nonprofits—often mid-sized agencies with limited access to new collateral—are actively seeking lower-cost, longer-term facilities.

Use Case 3 "Smoothing Out Lumpy" Seasonal Revenue

What we heard from nonprofits

A local community development organization receives nearly half of its annual contributions during the December "giving surge" but operates year-round on programmatic commitments. Previous attempts to secure a revolving credit line were declined due to limited collateral and uneven cash flow. This use case calls for a risk-tolerant credit facility permitting draws during lean months.

What the data says

Federal Reserve surveys of institutional lenders note that seasonal credit products are in high demand among any organization whose cash inflows cluster in particular months. CDFIs report fielding frequent inquiries from arts organizations, food banks, and other groups that need a revolving facility to tide them over until their next fundraising push.

Roundtable Session 2 — Lender Perspective

Most of Atlanta's mission-driven lenders maintain some level of engagement with nonprofits. Still, those relationships tend to reflect each institution's broader strategic focus rather than a singular commitment to the nonprofit sector. Commercial banks commonly serve nonprofits as depositors, providing checking, payroll, and cash-management services. Real estate-oriented CDFIs (e.g., affordable housing) routinely underwrite loans to nonprofit developers. Entrepreneurship-focused CDFIs concentrate on small businesses, with nonprofits often appearing only in niches like childcare.

What stands out in Atlanta's ecosystem is the absence of a lender whose primary mission and client base are exclusively nonprofits. No local CDFI exists today whose core charter is to underwrite working capital, bridge financing, or programmatic loans solely on behalf of mission-driven organizations.

Factors Limiting Greater In-Market Lending to Nonprofits

Collateral-First Underwriting
Non-depository lenders typically extend bridge and working-capital facilities only when secured by real estate or tangible collateral, maintaining conservative loss-reserve ratios.
Limited Operating Capital Interest
CDFIs noted limited interest in providing operating capital for routine cash flow issues—though instances exist when key foundation/local partner involvement de-risks the facility.
Pledged Deposit Models
Depositories can channel impact investor dollars into pledged (controlled) deposit accounts as guarantee backstops, raising their equity base and unlocking high-impact lending.
CDFI Sector Headwinds
CDFIs are also navigating uncertain waters due to federal funding threats, cuts, and departmental downsizing—directly affecting their weighted average cost of capital and loan pricing.

Ecosystem Transformation

Five layered ecosystem interventions to bring about a healthier, navigable nonprofit capital system.

Systems change requires layered solutions. Local foundations expressed the view that Atlanta's nonprofit lending environment has persistent gaps that prevent a healthy, navigable system for nonprofits to access capital. To bring about a different system, foundations must simultaneously identify core "quarterbacking" partners and mechanisms and invest in layered solutions & approaches.
Gap 1

Today's Gap

Limited Nonprofit Capital Readiness

Most nonprofits, particularly small to midsize organizations, lack the in-house financial expertise, systems, or capacity required to engage with debt financing. While many CDFIs offer light-touch TA as part of the lending process, these offerings are often insufficient to meaningfully change a nonprofit's financial position.

→

Proposed Intervention

Invest in Structured TA, Coaching & Loan Readiness Programs

Design and fund a robust nonprofit-focused technical assistance and coaching infrastructure that better positions nonprofits to examine their business and financial models. A structured, multi-modality educational learning and capacity-building program.

→

Future State

Centralized, coordinated, robust education & training resources

Plus a formalized "Nerve Center" for ongoing coordination.

Success Dependencies

  • A "nonprofit financial sustainability & resilience" framework to guide the learning curriculum and planning
  • A centralized educational calendar fed by multiple key organizing partners; strong cross-promotion & event marketing
  • Grant funding for educational partner programming expenses & a well-funded Nonprofit Sector Sustainability Fund at CFGA
  • Curated calendar of publicly available webinars & trainings; topical webinars for Georgia nonprofits; group and individualized coaching; loan readiness bootcamps; access to fractional CFO and back-office service providers
Gap 2

Today's Gap

Zero Dedicated Nonprofit-Serving Lenders

While CDFIs are often the most flexible lenders in the market, many are not structured or staffed to consistently underwrite nonprofit borrowers. Their charters often prioritize small business development, housing, or consumer finance. Many lack deep experience working with nonprofit financials and board governance dynamics.

→

Proposed Intervention

Recruit & Capitalize In-Market, Nonprofit-Focused Lending Partners

To address this structural gap, recruit and capitalize one or more lending institutions—either existing CDFIs or new entrants—to specialize in nonprofit working capital and programmatic lending.

→

Future State

New in-market CDFIs with dedicated nonprofit-tailored loan products & programs

Plus a deep "bench" of sources of capital.

Gap 3

Today's Gap

Fragmented Enhancement Tools

Existing credit enhancement and guarantee resources are scattered across the ecosystem, making it difficult for lenders to find and utilize them efficiently for nonprofit lending.

→

Proposed Intervention

Retool & Recapitalize the Community Guarantee Pool

To increase nonprofit access to loans (especially unsecured or bridge loans), funders capitalize a retooled tiered Community Guarantee Pool (CGP) at CFGA. Lower-risk tier continues 20% guarantees on state/local reimbursable grants; higher-risk tier covers two use cases: bridge lending for federal grant receivables, and non-collateralized working capital.

→

Future State

Centralized pool of credit enhancements & guarantee resources

Centralized flexible funding pool to cover capacity building & training, interest rate buy-down, etc.

Success Dependencies

  • Grants to replenish the Interest Rate Buy-Down mechanism for the Tier 1 guarantee tranche
  • Grants to establish a loan loss reserve Tier 2 tranche to offset potential losses
  • Grants to support additional CGP operational management duties—leverage CFGA's new Impact Investing Analyst for underwriting support
  • CFGA may execute MOUs with participating CDFIs to establish permissible loan types, nonprofit borrower profile, guarantee structures, and expectations
Gap 4

Today's Gap

Limited or Retreating Risk Tolerance

Many nonprofits face short-term cash flow shortfalls while waiting on government reimbursements, donor pledges, or delayed contract payments. These timing mismatches create real liquidity crises—leading to payroll stress, paused programs, or missed opportunities. Yet few flexible bridge loan products exist; traditional credit criteria don't work well in this context.

→

Proposed Intervention

Stand Up a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) for Nonprofit Bridge Lending

An SPV structure can serve as a nimble, standalone fund to deliver bridge loans to nonprofits, supported by philanthropic capital, recoverable grants, and CDFI participation.

→

Future State

Deep "bench" of sources of capital for nonprofits

Whether at existing CDFIs / in-market lenders or SPVs.

Gap 5

Today's Gap

Reactive, Crisis-Driven "Rush" to Coordinate

CFGA has been informally thrust into an ecosystem organizing role, but a more diffuse coordinated, networked approach—supported by but not anchored within CFGA—may result in more shared ownership and accountability from other players.

→

Proposed Intervention

Formalized, Engaged, Purposeful Leadership "Nerve Center"

A decentralized "nerve center" approach requires early, thoughtful vision alignment and governance discussions among committed partners.

→

Future State

Formalized "Nerve Center"

Pipeline development, promotion of resources, capacity building needs identification, new loan product & underwriting prototyping, capital aggregation, partner recruitment.

Success Dependencies

  • Grant/impact investment alignment with the recommendations (collectively or individually)
  • Referral pipeline for retooled CGP and for NFF/REDF product offerings
  • Design/implementation of SPV pilot fund for nonprofit bridge lending

Nonprofit Financial Sustainability Spectrum

A diagnostic framework for funders, CDFIs, and capacity builders to assess where nonprofits sit on the financial stability spectrum—and design targeted interventions that move the needle.

Purpose: Every nonprofit falls somewhere on the continuum from crisis to sustainability. Understanding these stages—and what support works at each level—is essential for deploying technical assistance and capital effectively. This isn't about judging organizations; it's about meeting them where they are with what they actually need.

Understanding Your Financial Position

Three quick diagnostics every nonprofit (and their funders) should know:

Days Cash on Hand Calculator
(Unrestricted Cash + Short-term Investments) ÷ (Annual Operating Expenses ÷ 365)
Georgia Benchmarks:
  • 21% have ≤30 days (crisis)
  • 35% have 31–90 days (vulnerable)
  • 29% have 91–180 days (building)
  • 15% have 180+ days (resilient)
Working Capital Health
Current Assets − Current Liabilities = Your cushion for cash flow gaps
Warning Signs:
  • Negative working capital
  • Can't cover payroll without new grants
  • Vendor payments consistently late
  • Using restricted funds for operations
Revenue Concentration Risk
What % comes from your largest funder? High concentration = high risk
Healthy Targets:
  • No single source >40%
  • Government funding <60%
  • 4+ diverse revenue streams
  • Some unrestricted revenue

Remember: This is a Diagnostic Tool, Not a Judgment

Organizations end up financially vulnerable for systemic reasons—decades of starvation funding, restricted grants that don't cover true costs, and funders' unwillingness to invest in infrastructure. The goal isn't to make every nonprofit look the same; it's to help each one build the financial resilience that fits their mission, model, and community.
