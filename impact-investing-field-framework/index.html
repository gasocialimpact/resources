IMPACT INVESTING FIELD & PORTFOLIO FRAMEWORK

Impact investing covers three top-level strategy families and six sub-strategies between them. The lines between families blur as investors move along the spectrum. Read the spectrum visualization to see how the families overlap, then open each strategy below to see its sub-strategies.

Incremental intent

Responsible Investing

Avoid harm. Set an impact floor across the existing portfolio.

Divesting and Negative ESG Screening

Selling or screening out stocks that are not ESG-aligned or are detrimental to the asset holder's mission. Usually applied across the entire portfolio.

ExampleA health-focused foundation instructs its investment manager to screen out alcohol, tobacco, and firearm stocks because of adverse health impacts.

Activist Investing (Shareholder Activism)

Buying stock in publicly traded companies to become a shareholder activist and promote an agenda aligned with the investor's mission.

ExampleAn institutional investor buys stock in a company to promote better workplace practices or board diversity through shareholder advocacy.
Return profile
Market rate
Typical access
Mostly indirect
Restorative intent

Values-Aligned Investing

Select positively for contribution. Articulate impact goals more precisely.

ESG Inclusion Investing

Buying stocks in companies that are highly aligned with ESG factors or values, such as diverse executive leadership or strong environmental stewardship.

ExampleAn investor allocates to a public equity fund that overweights companies with strong climate disclosure and diverse leadership.

Thematic Impact Investing

Using a particular thematic lens to invest across asset classes and return profiles to advance a targeted impact area like climate, health, or housing.

ExampleA donor-advised fund offers a clean energy investment pool that fundholders can elect to direct a percentage of their assets into.
Return profile
Market to near-market
Typical access
Mixed direct & indirect
Transformational intent

Impact-First Investing

Bring capital into direct relationship with mission, purpose, and community.

Catalytic Impact Investing

Tolerating greater risk, embracing more flexible structures, offering more patient capital terms, and accepting impact-adjusted financial rate of return.

ExampleA foundation provides a recoverable grant or first-loss capital so that a community development project can attract conventional senior debt.

Place-Based Impact Investing

Investing in local companies, organizations, or funds with the intention to generate measurable community benefit and financial returns.

ExampleAn investor seeds a new microlender to invest in local small businesses, or makes a bridge loan to a nonprofit awaiting government reimbursement.
Return profile
Below-market to recoverable
Typical access
Mostly direct
Guided by Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria guide decisions across Responsible and Values-Aligned strategies.
Guided by Mission, Purpose, and Founding Charter criteria guide Impact-First decisions.

Reading the strategies

The three categories form a progression. Responsible Investing focuses on the lens of avoiding harm and shaping company behavior. Values-Aligned Investing actively selects companies and themes that match what the investor stands for. Impact-First Investing prioritizes measurable community outcomes, with capital structured to support the investee rather than to maximize returns. Each investor can pick where to start and grow into deeper alignment over time.

Loans made directly from investor to end borrower without an intermediary. Includes loans to specific real estate projects, to non-intermediary nonprofits, and to social enterprises or mission-driven businesses.

Recoverable Grants
Grants structured to expect principal repayment if specified conditions are met. Function as zero-interest patient capital for predevelopment, capacity building, or proof of concept.

Contractual commitments to cover losses on senior debt so the senior layer can underwrite at conventional terms. Includes partial guarantees, letters of credit, and loan loss reserves.

A note on Program-Related Investments. PRIs are an IRS designation, not a vehicle. A PRI can be structured as debt, equity, deposits, or guarantees as long as it meets the IRS test (primary charitable purpose, no significant private benefit). Most of the Impact-First vehicles above can be issued or received as PRIs when a foundation is on one side of the transaction.

The same three strategies look different depending on whose seat you sit in or which impact area you have in view. Toggle between an allocator perspective and an impact sector perspective.

Individual investors

Everyday investors whose primary capital sits in brokerage accounts, retirement plans, and bank deposits. Most often access impact investing through pooled vehicles, with a growing set of direct options at low minimums.

Common constraints. Smaller dollar amounts per investment. Limited access to private placements. Choices often restricted to what an advisor or workplace retirement plan menu offers.
Incremental

Responsible

Pick ESG-screened mutual funds or ETFs in your brokerage or workplace retirement plan. Choose a sustainable robo-advisor. Move checking and savings to a community bank or credit union.

Typical tools
  • SRI mutual funds and ETFs in your 401(k) or IRA
  • Robo-advisors that pick sustainable funds
  • Move deposits to a community bank or credit union
Restorative

Values Aligned

Allocate into thematic ETFs and mutual funds. Buy a community investment note. Buy into an impact real estate fund at a low minimum. Use a donor-advised fund with thematic giving options.

Typical tools
  • Thematic ETFs (climate, gender, equity)
  • Community investment notes at low minimums
  • Impact REITs open to non-accredited investors
Transformational

Impact-First

Buy a direct loan to a community lender. Participate in a crowdfunded community investment for a local cooperative or community land trust. Become a member-owner at a credit union or food cooperative.

Typical tools
  • Direct investor notes to community lenders
  • Crowdfunded community investments
  • Credit union and food cooperative membership

Pension funds

Public and corporate pension plans, plus large workplace retirement plans. Operate under a strict legal duty to plan participants, which shapes how impact can be incorporated and how it must be framed.

Common constraints. Fiduciary duty to plan beneficiaries. Regulatory scrutiny. Scale requirements. Conservative investment policy norms. Multi-year cycles for adopting new mandates.
Incremental

Responsible

Add ESG factors to manager selection. Apply targeted exclusions tied to material risk. Set policies for how managers vote on shareholder issues.

Typical tools
  • ESG language in the investment policy
  • Stewardship and proxy voting guidelines
  • ESG questionnaires for asset managers
Restorative

Values Aligned

Set aside a piece of the portfolio for climate-aligned investments and sustainable infrastructure. Buy green and social bonds. Build dedicated thematic mandates where the investment policy allows.

Typical tools
  • Climate-aligned public equity mandate
  • Sustainable infrastructure fund commitments
  • Green and social bond allocations
Transformational

Impact-First

Limited but real. Some pensions carve out a small piece of the portfolio for investments that benefit the geography the pension serves, framed inside fiduciary duty as risk-adjusted return with local benefit.

Typical tools
  • In-state private equity and debt mandates
  • Workforce and affordable housing infrastructure
  • Targeted economic development partnerships

Foundations and mission endowments

Private and community foundations, plus mission-driven endowments. The institutions with the most flexibility on the spectrum because mission-aligned tools like Program-Related Investments and recoverable grants sit inside the legal toolbox.

Common constraints. Board governance cycles. Annual payout requirements. Internal capacity to underwrite non-standard deals. The gap between program staff and investment staff.
Incremental

Responsible

Update the investment policy with exclusions tied to mission. Add ESG integration language. Run shareholder engagement on a small set of priority issues.

Typical tools
  • Mission-aligned exclusion list
  • ESG integration into manager underwriting
  • Shareholder advocacy on priority issues
Restorative

Values Aligned

Allocate Mission-Related Investments from the endowment into thematic public and private vehicles. Build a dedicated thematic sleeve. Move endowment cash into mission-aligned banks and credit unions.

Typical tools
  • Mission-Related Investments from the endowment
  • Thematic private debt and private equity funds
  • Mission deposits at community-serving banks
Transformational

Impact-First

Run a Program-Related Investment strategy alongside the grantmaking pipeline. Issue recoverable grants for predevelopment. Take catalytic positions in funds. Anchor place-based capital stacks.

Typical tools
  • PRI portfolio (loans, equity, guarantees)
  • Recoverable grants for predevelopment
  • Catalytic positions in mission funds

High net worth investors and family offices

Wealthy households and the single and multi-family offices that serve them. Carry the broadest direct access to private market opportunities and the fastest paths to bespoke structures.

Common constraints. Family alignment across generations. Internal capacity to underwrite direct deals. Comfort with illiquidity. Vulnerability to advisor product preferences over independent thesis.
Incremental

Responsible

Customized investment accounts with family-specific exclusion lists. ESG-integrated private equity commitments. Proxy voting tied to family priorities.

Typical tools
  • Customized separately managed accounts
  • ESG-integrated private equity
  • Family-aligned proxy voting
Restorative

Values Aligned

Direct thematic positions in climate, gender, or place. Bespoke sustainable real assets. Co-investment alongside thematic fund managers. Mission deposit programs.

Typical tools
  • Direct thematic venture and growth equity
  • Co-investment with thematic fund managers
  • Bespoke sustainable real assets
Transformational

Impact-First

Direct catalytic positions in mission deals. Family foundation PRI strategy paired with grantmaking. Underwriting integrated capital stacks alongside mission developers.

Typical tools
  • Direct catalytic positions in mission deals
  • Family foundation PRI strategy
  • Bespoke integrated capital stacks

Faith-based institutions

Denominational pension funds, religious endowments, congregational investment committees, and faith-aligned investor networks. Carry the longest continuous history of values-based investing in the United States.

Common constraints. Denominational governance and review cycles. Doctrinal alignment requirements. Varied scale across congregations.
Incremental

Responsible

Apply faith-aligned exclusion screens grounded in denominational ethical investment guidelines. Run active shareholder advocacy through faith-based corporate responsibility coalitions.

Typical tools
  • Faith-aligned exclusion screens
  • Faith-based shareholder advocacy
  • Proxy voting policy tied to doctrine
Restorative

Values Aligned

Faith-screened thematic funds. Mission deposits at religious credit unions and community-serving banks. Climate-aligned allocations grounded in creation care priorities.

Typical tools
  • Faith-screened thematic mutual funds
  • Mission deposits at religious credit unions
  • Climate-aligned denominational pools
Transformational

Impact-First

Religious endowment PRIs paired with grantmaking. Faith-land housing partnerships. Congregational lending circles. Direct investments in community-rooted projects.

Typical tools
  • Religious endowment PRI strategy
  • Faith-land housing partnerships
  • Congregational lending circles

Anchor institutions (Eds and Meds)

Universities, college systems, hospitals, and health systems whose physical footprint, employment, and economic activity are rooted in a specific place. Uniquely able to do place-based impact at scale.

Common constraints. Endowment governance separate from operations and community affairs. Complex internal politics. Regulatory and accreditation pressure. Community benefit reporting for nonprofit hospitals.
Incremental

Responsible

Endowment SRI policy. Exclusion of weapons, fossil fuels, or other categories tied to institutional mission. ESG integration through the investment office.

Typical tools
  • Endowment SRI policy
  • Manager-level ESG integration
  • Sustainable purchasing policies in operations
Restorative

Values Aligned

Local procurement programs that shift institutional spending to local businesses. Place-based investments tied to mission. Health system community benefit investments in social determinants of health.

Typical tools
  • Local procurement programs
  • Place-based real estate investments
  • Community benefit investments in housing, food, child care
Transformational

Impact-First

Direct lending and investing to local businesses and developers. Worker cooperative incubation. Partnerships between hospitals and community lenders for housing, food access, and small business development.

Typical tools
  • Anchor mission investment program
  • Worker cooperative incubation
  • Hospital partnerships with community lenders

Corporate treasuries

Corporate cash management and balance sheet investments on the asset side, plus the workplace retirement and benefits programs that touch employee savings on the staff side. Both channels move large dollar amounts that most corporate executives never connect to impact.

Common constraints. Treasury risk policies oriented to liquidity and capital preservation. Fiduciary duty for workplace retirement plans. The gap between corporate sustainability strategy and treasury operations.
Incremental

Responsible

Corporate assets. ESG-screened cash management. ESG-integrated short-term bond and commercial paper holdings.

Staff benefits. ESG fund options in the workplace retirement plan menu.

Typical tools
  • Sustainable money market funds
  • ESG-integrated short-duration mandates
  • ESG fund options in 401(k) menus
Restorative

Values Aligned

Corporate assets. Mission deposits at community-serving banks for cash reserves. Green bond allocations.

Staff benefits. Climate-aligned target date funds. Thematic and gender-lens fund options in retirement plan menus.

Typical tools
  • Mission deposits at community-serving banks
  • Corporate green bond purchases
  • Climate-aligned target date funds in retirement plans
Transformational

Impact-First

Corporate assets. Community development deposit programs, often paired with Community Reinvestment Act goals. Corporate catalytic capital supporting workforce, supply chain, or community development.

Staff benefits. Self-directed brokerage windows that give employees access to mission options.

Typical tools
  • Community development deposit programs
  • Corporate catalytic capital for supplier development
  • Self-directed brokerage windows in retirement plans

Housing

Affordable rental, ownership, and shared housing structures. One of the largest and most developed sectors in impact investing, with a deep stack of tax credit, debt, equity, and community ownership instruments.

What this sector touches. Multifamily and single-family affordable rental. Permanent affordability. Homeownership pathways. Manufactured housing communities. Community ownership of land and buildings.
Incremental

Responsible

ESG-screened housing exposure through public REITs and ESG-integrated real estate funds. Exclusion of operators with predatory practices.

Typical tools
  • ESG-screened public REITs
  • Real estate ESG integration in private funds
  • Operator-level exclusion screens
Restorative

Values Aligned

Affordable housing financing at scale through tax credits, dedicated REITs, social bonds, and preservation funds. Where most of today's institutional affordable housing capital sits.

Typical tools
  • Low-Income Housing Tax Credit equity
  • Housing authority and agency social bonds
  • Affordable housing REITs and preservation funds
Transformational

Impact-First

Community ownership and place-rooted housing structures. Capital that supports permanent affordability and community governance, often through bespoke capital stacks.

Typical tools
  • Community land trusts
  • Resident-owned manufactured housing communities
  • Faith-land housing partnerships
  • Housing CDFI loans and predevelopment grants

Climate & Energy

Decarbonization, clean energy, and climate resilience finance. The fastest-growing sector across the spectrum in dollar volume, with deeply different vehicles at each tier.

What this sector touches. Renewable generation. Energy efficiency. Transmission and storage. Climate adaptation. Climate tech ventures. Community-owned energy.
Incremental

Responsible

Climate risk integration into public equity and fixed income manager selection. Fossil fuel exclusions. ESG-integrated infrastructure mandates.

Typical tools
  • Climate-risk integrated public equity
  • Fossil fuel exclusions
  • ESG infrastructure funds
Restorative

Values Aligned

Climate-aligned debt and equity at scale. Labeled bonds, tax equity, thematic VC and PE in climate solutions, and renewable infrastructure funds.

Typical tools
  • Green and sustainability-linked bonds
  • Investment Tax Credit equity for clean energy
  • Climate tech VC and energy transition PE
  • Renewable infrastructure funds
Transformational

Impact-First

Community-owned clean energy and climate justice finance. Capital that anchors generation and resilience assets in the communities most affected by climate transition costs and benefits.

Typical tools
  • Community-owned solar and utilities
  • Solar cooperative member equity
  • Green bank and climate CDFI investor notes
  • Recoverable grants for community climate projects

Business & Workforce

Inclusive entrepreneurship, small business growth, business transition finance, and worker ownership. Where the marketplace meets the engines of local employment and wealth creation.

What this sector touches. Diverse-founded startups and growth companies. Small business lending in underserved markets. Worker cooperatives and conversions. Business transition through ownership change.
Incremental

Responsible

ESG-integrated public equity with workforce diversity and supply chain criteria. Engagement on board diversity and worker practices.

Typical tools
  • ESG-screened public equity holdings
  • Workforce diversity screens
  • Supply chain ESG criteria
Restorative

Values Aligned

Inclusive business growth equity and tax credit-supported community business finance. Capital that backs underrepresented founders and businesses serving underserved markets.

Typical tools
  • Inclusive entrepreneurship VC
  • Gender-lens funds
  • New Markets Tax Credit equity
  • Thematic private debt for inclusive small business
Transformational

Impact-First

Worker ownership, business transition finance, and direct community business capital. Capital that supports new forms of ownership and reaches borrowers conventional credit will not.

Typical tools
  • Worker cooperative equity and conversions
  • Direct loans to mission-driven businesses
  • Recoverable grants for emerging social enterprises
  • CDFI lending to underserved small business

Place-Based

Capital that follows a specific geography to build community wealth. The lens that turns abstract impact investing into investing in a particular set of streets, neighborhoods, and people.

What this sector touches. Local economic development. Community-owned real estate and businesses. Place-based intermediaries. Mission deposits in local financial institutions. Anchor institution investment.
Incremental

Responsible

Place-conscious manager selection and operational policies. Compliance investments that meet regulatory requirements like the Community Reinvestment Act.

Typical tools
  • Local procurement programs
  • Community Reinvestment Act qualifying allocations
  • Geography-conscious manager selection
Restorative

Values Aligned

Place-based thematic capital deployed in service of a specific community. Mission deposits, place-based funds, and partnerships with local intermediaries.

Typical tools
  • Mission deposits at local community banks and MDIs
  • Place-based thematic funds
  • Local economic development partnerships
Transformational

Impact-First

Place-rooted ownership and direct local capital. Community-owned real estate, direct lending to local businesses, and integrated capital that knits anchor institutions, foundations, and community lenders together.

Typical tools
  • Community land trusts and community-owned real estate
  • Direct loans to local businesses and developers
  • Place-based integrated capital partnerships
  • Recoverable grants for community capacity

Land & Natural Resources

Working land, forestry, agriculture, and natural resource investment. The sector that holds the long-dated cash flows and conservation stewardship that other asset classes do not.

What this sector touches. Working forests. Farmland and food systems. Conservation easements. Producer cooperatives. Indigenous and community land stewardship.
Incremental

Responsible

ESG integration in farmland, forestry, and resource-related holdings. Sustainability screens on commodity exposures.

Typical tools
  • ESG-integrated farmland and forestry funds
  • Sustainability screens on commodity exposures
Restorative

Values Aligned

Sustainable real assets focused on environmental and stewardship outcomes. Working forests, regenerative farmland, and conservation-aligned land holdings.

Typical tools
  • Sustainable working forestry funds
  • Regenerative farmland funds
  • Conservation easements
Transformational

Impact-First

Community and indigenous land stewardship. Capital that supports new ownership structures and producer-led cooperatives, often with long timelines and patient terms.

Typical tools
  • Indigenous land trusts
  • Community-owned land cooperatives
  • Direct investment in producer cooperatives
  • Recoverable grants for land transition
